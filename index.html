About

Some people are driven by a specific thing. I'm driven by all of it — the need to understand, to build, to have an opinion that's actually mine. I'm Jean.

I was born and raised in South Auckland, in a tight-knit Catholic community built mostly by people who came from somewhere else to make something here. My parents were immigrants. They gave me a life they never had — and that's not something I've ever been able to take lightly.

I've always been someone who fixates. I'll find a new subject — design, a subculture, a craft, a way of seeing — and I won't let go until I understand it well enough to have an actual opinion on it. Not a borrowed one. My own. That's how I develop taste: obsessively, from the inside out.

The tension I've carried longest is imposter syndrome. The quiet, persistent belief that I don't quite belong in the rooms I'm in. It's made me sharper, more self-critical, harder to satisfy. I don't think I'd want to lose that entirely — but I'm learning to work with it rather than for it.

When our family restaurant Kairali was short-staffed during Covid, I stepped in without being asked — juggling school at the same time. Nobody handed me a framework for that. You figure it out, or things fall apart.

Architecture school came later. It gave language to something I already did instinctively: before I touch anything visual, I need to understand the whole system — the story, the emotion, what the work is supposed to make someone feel. I built my first clothing brand, Eternal Lov, that way. Every image, every design decision mapped before anything was created.

That's still how I work. The aesthetics are the end of the process, not the beginning.

Atelier Avexa
Built a luxury furniture and home decor studio from zero — identity, Shopify, shoot direction, full visual system. Ongoing as Art Director.
View Project

There was nothing when I started. No identity, no visual language, no reference point — just a brief to build a luxury furniture and home decor studio from the ground up.

The work was to define what the brand actually believed about space before anything was designed. Avexa isn't about filling rooms — it's about how a room makes you feel when it's been considered. That conviction had to live in every asset before a single product was shot.

The palette came from that tension: burgundy against black and white, greens sitting inside light birch and oak. Vibrant, but restrained. Colour that earns its place rather than announces itself. Getting that balance right was the hardest and most important creative decision — because if the palette was off, the whole premise of the brand collapsed.

The result is a full creative system — identity, Shopify, shoot direction, social, campaigns — all running off one visual logic. Ongoing as Art Director.

// social · content · growth · 2025
Lunet Coffee
The product was good — nobody could see it. Rebuilt the world around it, voice and all. 300% engagement growth in two months.
View Project

The product was good. The problem was nobody could see it.

Lunet was sitting on the shelf next to every other coffee brand, making the same gestures, speaking the same language. It had nothing that said this is yours to the person who actually cared about their coffee.

The instinct was simple: stop competing on the shelf and start building a world. Lunet wasn't for everyone — it was for people who genuinely enjoy coffee, who have a ritual around it, who notice the difference. The brand needed to reflect that person back at themselves.

That meant overhauling everything at once — the visual style, the tone, the way captions were written, the feeling a post left you with after you scrolled past it. Simple design. Considered framing. A voice that was premium without being cold. The aesthetic had one job: make you want to sit down and actually taste it.

300% engagement growth in two months. Increased foot traffic. People quoting the brand voice back — which is the only metric that really tells you something landed.

UX/UI Design
Self-directed interface work — different industries, different problems, same job: make people feel something before they've decided to.
View Project

No brief. No client. Just a question I kept coming back to: what does good design actually do for the person looking at it?

The work here is self-directed and deliberately varied — different industries, different problems, different audiences. The through-line is the same in each one: a great interface doesn't just look considered, it makes the person on the other side feel something. Landing pages that hold attention. Flows that don't make you think. Experiences that do the job of convincing someone before they've consciously decided to be convinced.

This portfolio is part of that practice. A CV tells you what I've done. A LinkedIn tells you where I've been. Neither of them shows you how I think — the obsessiveness, the system-building, the point of view behind every decision. That gap is exactly what this site is trying to close.

// product · clothing brand · founder · 2021–22
Eternal Lov
A clothing brand born from a question about love during Covid. Two chapters, eighteen months, closed in 2022 — kept the lessons.
View Project

This one was personal.

Eternal Lov started from a question I was sitting with during Covid — about our longing to be loved, to feel it, to hold onto it. The brand was built around that seeking. Not love as a destination but love as something we're always moving toward, always learning again.

Chapter 1 was soft. Pale blue, handwritten script, the words learning to lov again across the chest. Something that felt like a quiet admission rather than a statement. Chapter 2 went darker — washed charcoal, two figures holding each other in heat and colour, raw in a way the first chapter wasn't. The brand was finding its range.

I closed it in 2022. The market was saturated and I didn't yet have the tools or the audience to cut through it the way the work deserved. That's an honest answer, and I'd rather give it than pretend otherwise.

What it taught me has stayed: build the audience before you drop the product. A brand without a community is just clothing. And before you touch a single thing, map the story — the emotion, the feeling, what you want someone to carry with them after they've seen it.

That instinct started here. It still runs everything I do.
